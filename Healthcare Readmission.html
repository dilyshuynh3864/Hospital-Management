Healthcare Readmission Fairness Audit using XGBoost¶

To ensure both clinical predictive power and institutional equity, this study deploys an end-to-end framework that blends traditional statistical testing with modern machine learning and dedicated algorithmic fairness toolkits.

Workflow Overview¶

1. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

  • Operational Imbalance: Visualizing the target distribution (readmitted) exposes severe class scarcity, such as the ~9.6% baseline prevalence for early readmissions.
  • Data Sparsity Risks: Cross-tabulating demographic attributes like race against operational metrics like number_diagnoses uncovers critical sample drops (such as isolating a cohort of only 16 Asian patients), warning us about potential statistical noise and metrics volatility in minority subgroups.

I visualize the target distribution (readmitted) to expose severe class scarcity and cross-tabulate demographic attributes like race against number_diagnoses. This allows me to immediately isolate critical sample drops in minority groups and flag potential statistical noise before downstream processing.

2. Statistical Modeling (OLS + ANOVA)

  • Evaluate if resource consumption scales proportionally with clinical complexity using regression frameworks.
  • Perform analysis of variance to mathematically prove whether variations in care are driven by objective medical necessity or demographic partitioning.

I construct linear regression models to evaluate whether resource consumption scales proportionally with clinical complexity. Additionally, I run an analysis of variance to mathematically prove whether variations in care are driven by objective medical necessity or influenced by demographic partitioning, setting a baseline standard for institutional equity.

3. XGBoost Classification

  • Map non-linear relationships and high-dimensional spaces using an extreme gradient boosting ensemble.
  • Utilize native tree-splitting optimizations and inject sample weights to mitigate minority class under-detection.

I programmatically cast features to categorical types to utilize native tree-splitting optimizations and bypass the dimensionality issues of one-hot encoding. To prevent minority class under-detection, I inject a progressive $X10$ loss penalty directly into the training sample weights to prioritize acute early readmissions.

4. Fairness Auditing with Fairlearn

  • Train the predictive ensemble blindly by withholding sensitive attributes from the active feature matrix.
  • Post-audit prediction arrays across demographic slices to evaluate performance consistency and parity boundaries.

I train the ensemble blindly by withholding sensitive variables from the active feature matrix to eliminate direct disparate treatment. I then pass the frozen prediction arrays through Fairlearn's MetricFrame to post-audit accuracy, selection rates, and false-negative rate disparities across demographic slices.

Empirical Distribution Diagnostics & Data Sparsity Risks¶

Based on the quantitative metrics derived from the cross-tabulation matrix across 49,985 total encounters, we conduct a structural audit of sample distributions to define risks for downstream statistical and machine learning modeling.

1. Sample Dominance & The Law of Large Numbers¶

The historical baseline is heavily dominated by the Caucasian cohort (39,109 samples) and the African American cohort (9,447 samples). From a data-auditing perspective, these large-scale footprints provide a robust mathematical foundation. By the Law of Large Numbers, the error variance of arithmetic estimators for these two segments shrinks toward zero, ensuring that downstream performance metrics reflect genuine systemic trends rather than random operational noise.

2. Mathematical Proof of Convergence at Diagnosis Level 9¶

The high-density focus matrix at number_diagnoses = 9 (the peak clinical complexity tier) explicitly validates near-perfect distributional alignment across all demographic slices:

  • Caucasian: 18,724 samples $\rightarrow$ Yields a stable Median of 49.0 lab procedures (IQR: 34.0 – 62.0)
  • African American: 3,633 samples $\rightarrow$ Yields a stable Median of 48.0 lab procedures (IQR: 37.0 – 61.0)
  • Hispanic: 395 samples $\rightarrow$ Yields a stable Median of 48.0 lab procedures (IQR: 34.0 – 63.0)
  • Asian: 141 samples $\rightarrow$ Yields a stable Median of 45.0 lab procedures (IQR: 30.0 – 61.0)

Auditing Insight: The data mathematically proves that when sample sizes are sufficiently dense, the variance between majority and minority medians collapses to a negligible margin ($\Delta \le 4.0$ procedures). This demonstrates that when adequate data is present, the healthcare network inherently exhibits systemic equity in diagnostic workflows.

3. Critical Sample Scarcity and Auditing Risks at Diagnosis Level 3¶

Conversely, the matrix exposes severe data sparsity at number_diagnoses = 3, capturing a critical system-wide low of only 65 Hispanic patients and 16 Asian patients:

  • Hispanic ($n = 65$): Distorted Median of 40.0 (IQR: 23.0 – 54.0)
  • Asian ($n = 16$): Highly volatile Median of 37.0 (IQR: 18.0 – 45.0)

Auditing Warning: A sample size of $n = 16$ fundamentally breaks down the stability of frequentist metrics. If just 1 or 2 isolated minority patients present unmeasured compounding acute conditions requiring intensive lab tracking, the entire subgroup's median artificially spikes or shifts. This extreme scarcity warns us that surface-level conclusions about "institutional bias" drawn purely from raw minority averages at the tails of the distribution lack mathematical validity.

1. Central Tendency & Clinical Resource Scaling¶

The boxplot matrix demonstrates a highly consistent, upward visual trend: as the medical complexity (number_diagnoses) shifts from 3 to 9, the overall Interquartile Ranges (IQRs) and median lines for all racial groups translate upward proportionally. At number_diagnoses = 3, the core median across groups resides at a lower tier of ~37–40 lab procedures, whereas at number_diagnoses = 9, the entire resource distribution shifts significantly upward to a median of ~45–49 lab procedures. This visually proves that clinical diagnostic workflows and resource allocations are strictly driven by the severity of the patient's condition rather than demographic partitioning.

2. Visual Geometric Convergence¶

At the highest severity level (number_diagnoses = 9), the four boxes representing each demographic group lie nearly parallel, displaying almost identical horizontal midpoints and vertical spread boundaries. This tight visual overlap across all four distribution shapes highlights that the healthcare system achieves an inherently consistent and fair standard of care across all patient populations when the operational baseline has access to sufficient data.

3. Outlier Distortion & Visual Volatility¶

The erratic geometric shapes observed in the minority cohorts at lower diagnosis tiers—such as the compressed boxes or sudden median shifts for the Asian group—do not reflect systemic institutional bias. Graphically, when a subgroup lacks a robust data spread, the presence of isolated upper outliers (with several patients reaching over 100+ lab procedures) forcefully pulls the distribution boundaries. This creates misleading "visual noise" on a chart rather than pointing to a verified pattern of discrimination.

Key Statistical Findings¶

1. The Clinical Baseline ($C(\text{number\_diagnoses})$)¶

With an $F$-statistic of $F(6, 49957) = 249.74$ and a $p$-value effectively hitting absolute zero ($p \approx 0$ or $10^{-316}$), the number of comorbid diagnoses is established as the dominant driver of lab test volume. This confirms robust clinical integrity across the institution: As a patient's medical complexity increases, the volume of diagnostic procedures scales up naturally across the board.

2. The Equity Indicator ($C(\text{race})$)¶

Even when controlling for medical severity (how sick a patient is), race exerts a highly significant independent effect on the number of lab procedures assigned ($F(3, 49957) = 28.61, p = 1.77 \times 10^{-18}$). The near-zero $p$-value proves that the baseline resource allocation variance between demographic groups is systemic and mathematically non-random, validating the necessity for an algorithmic fairness audit.

3. The Structural Shift ($C(\text{race}):C(\text{number\_diagnoses})$)¶

  • The Mathematical Fact: Unlike unadjusted preliminary tracking, the final balanced OLS model demonstrates that the interaction effect between race and clinical severity is not statistically significant ($F(18, 49957) = 1.47, p = 0.0893 > 0.05$).
  • The True Insight: Because we fail to reject the null hypothesis for this interaction term, the data proves that the resource gap driven by race does not dynamically change, compound, or worsen as a patient becomes sicker. Instead, the disparity remains relatively constant and parallel across all severity levels. This mathematically confirms that the sharp volatility (the "ups and downs") observed earlier in the Asian and Hispanic boxplots was purely statistical noise caused by small sample sizes rather than a shifting pattern of medical bias.
